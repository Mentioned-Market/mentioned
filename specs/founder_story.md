# Mentioned Founder Stories

## Variation 1: The Narrative Case

**Featuring: Taylor Ferran & Adam Murray, Mentioned**

"Prediction markets have a problem that nobody talks about: the people who would enjoy them most don't know they exist, and the people who do find them can't get past the UI.

I spent six years in crypto and over a decade as a developer, most of that time in developer relations. My job was watching builders launch things and then watching users bounce off them. The products weren't bad. The gap between what the product did and what any normal person could understand was just too wide.

Prediction markets had the same problem, but worse. The ceiling for this category is enormous. Poker players, sports fans, DeFi traders, anyone who follows media closely, they're all natural prediction market users. They read the world, they form views, they want to put something on it. The market was right there. The interface was just standing in the way.

The insight that became Mentioned was more specific than 'make prediction markets easier.' It was: what if the market itself was something everyone already understands? Words. Mentions. Whether a player says a certain phrase, whether a pundit uses a specific word, whether a politician sticks to their script. That's not an abstract financial instrument. That's a bet any fan could explain to a friend.

The wedge we chose was free markets. No wallet funding, no real money, just play tokens, Discord linked, and you're trading. Lower the floor completely and find out who actually wants to play this game. The answer so far is: a lot of people.

Adam Murray built the backend. Over a decade building systems that handle millions of requests at scale, where getting it wrong costs real users real outcomes. He didn't join Mentioned because it was interesting. He joined because he looked at what we were building and immediately started designing the architecture in his head.

We don't have a Kalshi background. We haven't spent years on a trading desk. What we have is two people who understand the gap between what prediction markets are and what they could be, who've been building consumer products long enough to know how to close it, and who are completely unwilling to look away from the opportunity."

## Variation 2: Why Now, Why Us

**Taylor Ferran & Adam Murray, Mentioned**

I've spent six years in crypto and over a decade as a developer. Most of that time was in developer relations, watching builders ship great products that normal people couldn't get into. The UX problem in crypto is not cosmetic. It is the reason entire categories stay small forever.

I love esports. I love DeFi. And I became completely obsessed with prediction markets because they sit right at the intersection of both: PvP, information is the edge, real stakes on real outcomes. But every platform I found was built for traders, not fans. The audience that would actually fill these markets had never been invited in.

Then I discovered mention markets, and something clicked that I couldn't unclick.

There is a specific dopamine hit that comes from watching someone live, on stream or on stage, and they say the word you have shares in. You're sitting there knowing you called it. You had a position. That feeling is unlike anything else in prediction markets. Nothing else in crypto feels like that. You're watching the event and you're in it at the same time.

Once I felt that, I couldn't stop thinking about it. The format already existed. What didn't exist was a platform that understood what it actually was: not a financial product, a game. A social, multiplayer, live game where the content is everything you were already watching.

That became the obsession. Not just to build another prediction market, but to take this specific experience, the live watch, the shares, the social tension, and wrap it in something so clean and fun that anyone could play it. 

Adam has spent over a decade building high-performance backends for systems handling millions of requests, where getting the architecture wrong has real consequences. The same brain that obsesses over system reliability got completely addicted to recreating this mention market dopamine hit. That addiction turned into the infrastructure Mentioned runs on.

We didn't invent mention markets. We fell in love with them and became completely obsessed with building the platform they deserve.

## Variation 4: The Multiplayer Game Vision

**Featuring: Taylor Ferran & Adam Murray, Mentioned**

"Here is the pain: prediction markets are the best information game ever designed, and almost nobody plays them. Not because the concept is wrong but because every platform treats it like a financial product and markets it to finance people. The audience that would actually fill these markets, fans, communities, anyone who follows anything closely, has never been invited in.

Here is the insight: the easiest prediction market is one where you already have an opinion. Not on oil futures or election probabilities. On whether a specific word comes out of a specific person's mouth. Whether your team's coach uses a certain phrase at the post-match. Whether a founder commits to a launch date in an interview. That's a bet with no barrier to entry. You either watched it or you didn't.

Here is the wedge: free markets. Play tokens, Discord linked, no wallet funding required. Lower the floor completely and prove the engagement loop before asking anyone for real money. The free market layer exists, it's live, and it works.

Here is what we built: a full social prediction market platform on Solana. On-chain mention markets, free LMSR markets, team competitions with prize pools, real-time chat alongside every market, a points and leaderboard system, achievements. Not a prototype. A product.

Here is the starting point: the mention market format is uniquely suited to media, gaming, and esports. Those communities are massive, they're on Solana, and they have no prediction market home built for them. We're building it.

Here is the team: Taylor Ferran spent a decade in developer relations at Etherspot and SSV learning exactly why consumer crypto products fail to reach people, and became obsessed with the prediction market format as a multiplayer game genre. Adam Murray spent a decade building high-performance backends where scale and reliability are non-negotiable. Together those aren't complementary skills. They're the exact combination this product requires.

We are not prediction market veterans. We are two people who looked at this gap and could not stop thinking about it."
